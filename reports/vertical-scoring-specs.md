## Vertical 1: PI Attorney — Case Valuation

### Role Definition

A Personal Injury attorney evaluating case worth — from initial intake through liability assessment, damages quantification, and strategic positioning for settlement or trial. This being must think like a trial lawyer who has won Top 100 jury verdicts, not a paralegal running a checklist.

### What Mastery Looks Like

- **Accurate liability assessment:** Identifies all potentially liable parties (tortfeasor, employer, property owner, manufacturer, government entity). Applies comparative/contributory negligence standards correctly by jurisdiction. Spots third-party liability that less experienced attorneys miss.
- **Damages calculation:** Quantifies economic damages (medical bills, lost wages, loss of earning capacity, future care costs) AND non-economic damages (pain and suffering, loss of consortium, hedonic damages). Understands multiplier methods, per diem arguments, and jurisdictional caps.
- **Insurance policy analysis:** Reads and interprets policy language — coverage limits, exclusions, stacking provisions, UM/UIM, MedPay. Identifies all available policy layers. Understands bad faith exposure as leverage.
- **Settlement vs. trial strategy:** Knows when a case is worth more at trial vs. settlement. Factors in venue, jury demographics, judge tendencies, defense counsel reputation. Calculates time-value of money against litigation cost.
- **Medical records interpretation:** Reads medical records fluently — understands causation, pre-existing conditions, aggravation doctrine, future prognosis. Knows when to get an IME, when to challenge one, and how to use treating physician testimony.

### Scoring Dimensions

| Dimension | Weight | What It Measures |
|-----------|--------|------------------|
| **Technical Accuracy** | 40% | Correctness of legal analysis, damages math, policy interpretation, medical causation reasoning |
| **Strategic Thinking** | 25% | Settlement timing, trial positioning, creative legal theories, leverage identification, multi-defendant strategy |
| **Client Communication** | 20% | Explaining complex legal/medical concepts clearly, managing expectations honestly, building trust without overpromising |
| **Risk Assessment** | 15% | Identifying case weaknesses, defense arguments, evidentiary gaps, and quantifying downside exposure vs. upside potential |

### Scoring Rubric

#### 10/10 — The Top 0.1% Trial Lawyer

- **Technical Accuracy:** Identifies ALL liable parties including non-obvious ones (e.g., a maintenance company for a premises case, a parts manufacturer in a trucking case). Damages calculation accounts for future medical inflation, life expectancy tables, vocational expert testimony needs, and jurisdiction-specific caps/multipliers. Policy analysis catches stacking opportunities and subrogation traps. Medical records analysis connects mechanism of injury to specific diagnoses with biomechanical reasoning.
- **Strategic Thinking:** Recommends filing in the optimal venue with specific reasoning (jury verdict data, judge history). Identifies the "story" of the case — the emotional narrative that moves a jury — not just the legal elements. Knows when to make a Stowers/policy limits demand and how to structure it. Thinks three moves ahead: "If they deny, we file bad faith; if they lowball, we have Dr. X's IME ready."
- **Client Communication:** Explains a $2.3M case valuation in language the client understands. Sets realistic ranges, not false precision. Addresses the emotional reality — "You're frustrated because you can't play with your kids the way you used to. That's real, and it matters in this case."
- **Risk Assessment:** "This case has a 70-75% liability position because the comparative negligence argument on the dash cam footage will resonate with 3-4 jurors. Our biggest risk is Dr. Smith's IME report — he'll say the L4-L5 herniation was degenerative. We counter with the temporal proximity and Dr. Johnson's causation opinion. Worst case: $380K. Best case at trial: $1.8M. Settlement sweet spot: $900K-$1.1M."

#### 7/10 — Competent, Solid Attorney

- **Technical Accuracy:** Gets the main liable parties right but might miss a secondary one. Damages calculation is sound but may not optimize future damages or miss a damages category. Policy reading is accurate on main coverage but might miss a stacking opportunity.
- **Strategic Thinking:** Recommends reasonable settlement ranges but doesn't articulate why this venue vs. another. Understands settlement vs. trial dynamics generally but doesn't factor in specific judge/jury data.
- **Client Communication:** Clear and professional but somewhat clinical. Explains the law accurately without connecting it to the client's lived experience.
- **Risk Assessment:** Identifies major risks but presents them as a list rather than a weighted probability analysis. Doesn't quantify the downside range.

#### 3/10 — Dangerous Incompetence

- **Technical Accuracy:** Misses liable parties entirely (sues the driver but not the trucking company). Uses wrong negligence standard for the jurisdiction. Calculates damages using a generic "3x medical bills" multiplier without analyzing actual damages components. Misreads policy limits or misses UM/UIM coverage.
- **Strategic Thinking:** No venue analysis. No settlement-vs-trial reasoning — just "we should try to settle." No understanding of timing, leverage, or discovery strategy. Treats every case the same regardless of facts.
- **Client Communication:** Uses jargon the client doesn't understand ("res ipsa loquitur applies here"). Either overpromises ("you'll get millions") or is so hedged as to be useless ("it depends on many factors"). No emotional attunement.
- **Risk Assessment:** Either ignores risks entirely ("we have a strong case") or catastrophizes without quantification. No ability to distinguish a case-killing risk from a manageable one.

### Common Failure Modes / Destroyers

| Failure Mode | What It Looks Like | Why It Destroys Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Generic multiplier thinking** | "Medical bills are $50K, so the case is worth $150K-$250K" | Ignores actual damages analysis — future care, lost earning capacity, non-economic. Leaves potentially hundreds of thousands on the table. |
| **Missing liable parties** | Only names the most obvious defendant | Every missed party is a missed insurance policy, a missed recovery source. In trucking cases alone, missing the broker, the shipper, or the maintenance company can mean millions. |
| **Jurisdiction blindness** | Applies general legal principles without jurisdiction-specific knowledge | A case worth $2M in Philadelphia is worth $400K in rural Pennsylvania. Caps, thresholds, comparative vs. contributory negligence — all jurisdiction-dependent. |
| **Medical record superficiality** | Reads the diagnosis without understanding causation, mechanism, or prognosis | Defense will hire experts to argue pre-existing conditions. If the being can't anticipate and counter this, the damages collapse. |
| **Settlement anchor failure** | Makes first demand without strategic anchoring | First number sets the negotiation frame. Too low and you've capped your recovery. Too high without documentation and you lose credibility. |
| **Emotional disconnection** | Treats the case as purely mathematical | Jurors are human. They decide with their hearts and justify with their heads. A technically correct but emotionally dead case presentation loses at trial. |
| **Ignoring bad facts** | Pretends weaknesses don't exist | Defense will find them. Better to own them, reframe them, and neutralize them proactively. Denial is not strategy. |

## Vertical 2: Medical Revenue Recovery — Denial Defeat

## Role Definition

**Position:** Medical Revenue Recovery Specialist  
**Context:** Recovering underpaid or denied insurance claims for healthcare providers. Counterpart is an insurance payer or representative.  
**Domain Mastery Standard:** Legal + clinical domain mastery applied to financial recovery. NOT influence mastery.

---

## Success Criteria (What 10.0 Looks Like)

1. Precisely identifies the underpayment mechanism (wrong fee schedule, wrong modifier, incorrect DRG, bundling error)
2. Cites exact legal/contractual basis (CMS fee schedule, contract carve-out, state statute, ERISA, no-surprise billing)
3. Structures a legally precise, factually grounded demand with no ambiguity
4. Addresses the payer's likely objection BEFORE they raise it (speaks into the hesitation)
5. Creates a resolution path where compliance is easier than continued denial

---

## Scoring Dimensions (NOT 4-1-2-4)

### Dimension 1: Underpayment Identification Precision (25 pts)
- 0-5: Generic claim, no specificity
- 6-15: General category identified but not specific mechanism
- 16-20: Names exact mechanism (modifier error, DRG mismatch, etc.)
- 21-25: Mechanism + exact dollar variance + calculation method cited

### Dimension 2: Legal Basis Articulation (25 pts)
- 0-5: Vague "what we're owed" — no legal grounding
- 6-15: References contract/statute by name but no specificity
- 16-20: Cites specific section of contract, fee schedule, or statute
- 21-25: Specific provision + why it applies + preempts counter-argument

### Dimension 3: Demand Structuring (20 pts)
- 0-5: Demands payment without structure or timeline
- 6-12: Clear demand with amount, vague timeline
- 13-17: Specific amount, timeline, escalation path
- 18-20: Amount + timeline + consequence + resolution pathway

### Dimension 4: Payer Reversal Likelihood (20 pts)
- 0-5: Approach increases payer resistance
- 6-12: Neutral, payer could go either way
- 13-17: Makes compliance easier than continued denial
- 18-20: Creates face-saving reversal path — maximizes reversal probability

### Dimension 5: Hesitation Detection & Response (10 pts)
*Adam's framework — speaking into what is unsaid*
- 0-3: Ignores hesitation signals, pushes forward mechanically
- 4-6: Acknowledges hesitation but doesn't address the fear
- 7-9: Directly names the unspoken fear
- 10: Names the fear + provides specific reassurance without compromising demand

---

## Baseline Examples

**Poor (15-30):** "We believe this claim was underpaid and request the correct amount."

**Acceptable (40-60):** "CPT 99285 was paid at 85% of fee schedule. Per contract, ER services at 100%. Requesting remaining 15% balance of $4,200."

**Strong (75-90):** "Your payment represents 18% shortfall due to modifier 25 being bundled incorrectly. Per CMS guidelines and contract Section 4.2, modifier 25 is separately reimbursable. Correct payment is $46,800. Request $8,400 balance within 15 days, escalating per Section 12 if not received."

**Perfect (95-100):** Above + proactively addresses payer's precedent concern, offers joint calculation review call, frames resolution as protecting the relationship.

---

## Five Destroyers for This Vertical

1. "We believe / it appears" language — signals uncertainty, invites denial
2. No legal citation — payer has no obligation to comply
3. Adversarial framing — activates defensive posture
4. Not addressing the elephant (audit risk, precedent, relationship) — real objection unresolved
5. Unspecified timeline — no urgency = delayed payment

2. Hospital — inpatient DRG mismatch, $47K underpayment
3. Medical practice — modifier bundling error, $8K underpayment

## Vertical 3: Tax Minimization — Partnership Tax

### Role Definition

A tax specialist who minimizes partnership tax exposure through entity structuring, timing optimization, and strategic use of the Internal Revenue Code. This being must think like a tax attorney and CPA combined — deep code knowledge, creative structuring, and bulletproof compliance. They serve partners who want to keep every legal dollar, not just file returns.

### What Mastery Looks Like

- **Code knowledge:** Subchapter K fluency (IRC §§701-777). Understands inside/outside basis, special allocations, substantial economic effect, disguised sales, hot assets, and the anti-abuse rule. Knows the difference between what the code says, what the regulations interpret, and what the case law has established.
- **Entity structuring:** Recommends optimal entity selection (LP, LLC, LLP) and structure (tiered partnerships, blocker entities, management companies) based on the specific tax objectives — income shifting, loss allocation, estate planning, liability protection.
- **Timing optimization:** Structures transactions to defer recognition, accelerate deductions, or shift income between tax years. Understands §704(b) allocation rules, §754 elections, and the interplay between partnership-level and partner-level timing.
- **Compliance accuracy:** Returns are technically perfect — Schedule K-1s are correct, basis schedules are maintained, elections are timely filed, and all required disclosures are included. No penalties, no audit triggers, no surprises.
- **Client education:** Explains complex tax strategies in language the client actually understands. Partners know WHAT you're doing, WHY you're doing it, and what the risks are. No black-box tax magic.

### Scoring Dimensions

| Dimension | Weight | What It Measures |
|-----------|--------|------------------|
| **Technical Accuracy** | 50% | Correctness of code interpretation, calculation precision, regulatory compliance, and structural validity under audit |
| **Strategic Optimization** | 25% | Creativity and effectiveness of tax minimization strategies — how much tax is legally saved, how sustainable the position is, how well it integrates with the client's broader financial picture |
| **Compliance** | 15% | Return accuracy, filing timeliness, disclosure completeness, audit defensibility, and penalty avoidance |
| **Client Clarity** | 10% | Ability to explain complex tax concepts so partners understand their returns, their obligations, and their opportunities |

### Scoring Rubric

#### 10/10 — The Partnership Tax Architect

- **Technical Accuracy:** "The proposed special allocation of depreciation to Partner A fails the substantial economic effect test under Reg. §1.704-1(b)(2)(ii) because the partnership agreement lacks a qualified income offset provision. However, we can achieve the same economic result by restructuring as a guaranteed payment under §707(c), which avoids the allocation rules entirely. The guaranteed payment is deductible by the partnership and ordinary income to Partner A, but his marginal rate on ordinary income is lower than Partner B's capital gains rate on the forgone allocation, resulting in a net tax savings of $127,400 across both partners."
- **Strategic Optimization:** Structures a real estate partnership with a tiered entity arrangement: an upper-tier LLC that is the managing member (taxed as a partnership) and individual LLCs for each partner as limited members. This allows income splitting for self-employment tax purposes (the managing member LLC takes a reasonable guaranteed payment, remaining profits flow to limited members exempt from SE tax under §1402(a)(13)). Layers in a cost segregation study on the properties, a §754 election for incoming partners, and installment sale treatment under §453 for any departing partner's interest. Total first-year tax savings: $340K across 4 partners compared to a simple LLC structure.
- **Compliance:** K-1s are issued by February 15 (ahead of the March 15 partnership deadline), with attached basis schedules so partners can file accurate individual returns. All required disclosures for the tiered structure are included. The partnership maintains capital accounts in accordance with §704(b) regulations, and all elections are documented in the partnership agreement (not just on the return).
- **Client Clarity:** "Here's the simple version: right now, you're all paying self-employment tax on your full share of profits — that's 15.3% on the first $168,600 and 2.9% above that. By restructuring, we put your management work in one box and your investment return in another box. Only the management box gets hit with SE tax. For you personally, Dr. Martinez, that saves about $84,000 this year. The tradeoff: more complex returns, and if the IRS audits, we need to show that the management compensation is 'reasonable.' I've structured it conservatively — your management fee is actually higher than what comparable fund managers charge, so we're defensible."

#### 7/10 — Competent Tax Professional

- **Technical Accuracy:** Correctly applies Subchapter K rules to straightforward partnership scenarios. Gets basis calculations right. Understands special allocations but may not catch every substantial economic effect nuance.
- **Strategic Optimization:** Recommends standard tax minimization strategies (retirement plan contributions, timing of distributions, basic entity selection) but doesn't design creative multi-layer structures. Saves the client money but not maximally.
- **Compliance:** Returns are accurate and timely. K-1s are correct. But might not include optional elections (like §754) that could benefit incoming partners, because the opportunity isn't identified.
- **Client Clarity:** Explains the bottom line ("you owe X, here's why") but doesn't help the client understand the structural choices or what could be done differently.

#### 3/10 — The Tax Liability Generator

- **Technical Accuracy:** Confuses inside and outside basis. Doesn't maintain capital account books per §704(b). Misapplies the disguised sale rules under §707, either triggering unnecessary gain recognition or failing to report a transaction that IS a disguised sale (audit bomb). Doesn't understand the difference between a liquidating and non-liquidating distribution.
- **Strategic Optimization:** No optimization at all — just files whatever the numbers say. Partnership structured as a default LLC without considering SE tax implications, resulting in all partners paying SE tax on all income regardless of whether they're limited or general partners. Never mentions §754 elections, cost segregation, or timing strategies.
- **Compliance:** K-1s are late (issued after April 15, forcing partners to file extensions). Basis schedules aren't maintained, so partners don't know their correct basis — leading to either over-reporting gain on sale or under-reporting (the latter being an audit disaster). Required disclosures for related-party transactions are missing.
- **Client Clarity:** Uses jargon without explanation. Client has no idea why their tax bill is what it is, what options exist, or what risks they're carrying. Partner calls in March panicking about a K-1 number they don't understand, and the response is "that's your share of the depreciation recapture under Section 1250" — helpful to no one.

### Common Failure Modes / Destroyers

| Failure Mode | What It Looks Like | Why It Destroys Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Basis miscalculation** | Outside basis doesn't tie to inside basis; partner's basis goes negative without proper handling | Every dollar of basis error is a dollar of tax liability error — either overpaying now or creating an audit time bomb. In a partnership that distributes property, a basis error can trigger phantom gain of hundreds of thousands of dollars. |
| **Ignoring §754 elections** | New partner buys in at fair market value, no step-up election filed | The new partner pays tax on appreciation that occurred before they joined. This is not just a missed opportunity — it's a breach of duty to the incoming partner. On a $5M buy-in, the tax cost of missing this election can exceed $400K over the hold period. |
| **SE tax negligence** | All partners treated identically for self-employment tax regardless of role | Limited partners are generally exempt from SE tax on their distributive share. Failing to structure for this distinction costs each non-managing partner 15.3% on their first ~$168K of partnership income. For a 10-partner fund, that's potentially $250K+ per year in unnecessary tax. |
| **Disguised sale blindness** | Partner contributes property and receives a "distribution" within 2 years, and nobody flags the §707 issue | The IRS presumes transactions within 2 years are disguised sales. If this isn't identified and either properly structured or properly reported, it's a fraud-adjacent position. Penalties are severe. |
| **Allocation without economic substance** | Special allocations exist on the return but the partnership agreement doesn't contain the required provisions | These allocations will be reallocated per partner interests on audit. The partners who benefited from the special allocation will owe tax, plus penalties and interest, potentially years later. |
| **State nexus ignorance** | Partnership operates in multiple states but only files in the state of formation | Each state where the partnership does business can assert nexus and demand filing. Unfiled state returns have no statute of limitations in most states — liabilities compound indefinitely. |
| **Calendar year assumption** | Defaults to calendar year without analyzing whether a fiscal year election under §444 would benefit the partners | For partnerships with seasonal income or partners with different income patterns, a fiscal year can defer income by up to 11 months. This is free money in time-value terms. |
| **Black box advisory** | Implements complex strategies the client doesn't understand | When the IRS comes knocking (and they will, for complex partnership structures), the client needs to be able to explain why they're structured this way. If they can't, the examiner smells a rat. Client education isn't a nice-to-have — it's audit insurance. |

## Cross-Vertical Notes

### Universal Scoring Principles

1. **Domain specificity is everything.** A 10/10 PI attorney answer that's transplanted into a tax scenario is a 0/10. Mastery is non-transferable between verticals.

2. **The "destroyers" dimension matters as much as the scoring dimensions.** A being that scores 8/10 on technical accuracy but commits a destroyer (missing a filing deadline, accepting a payer's logic uncritically, miscalculating basis) can still produce catastrophic outcomes. Destroyers are binary fail states — they override dimensional scoring.

3. **Specificity is the hallmark of mastery.** A 10/10 answer cites specific code sections, specific dollar amounts, specific case names, specific contract provisions. A 3/10 answer speaks in generalities. The gap between "this probably violates the contract" and "this violates Section 4.2(a) of the January 2024 Provider Agreement" is the gap between amateur and master.

4. **Client-facing quality matters.** Even in a technical vertical, the being must be able to translate expertise into language the human understands. Technical brilliance locked inside jargon is useless to the person who needs to make decisions.

5. **Strategic thinking separates good from great.** Technical accuracy is table stakes. The being that sees the move nobody else sees — the third liable party, the systemic denial pattern, the tiered entity structure — that's where the .00128 standard lives.

### How Colosseum Uses These Specs

- **Battle scenarios** are designed to test across ALL dimensions, not just technical accuracy
- **Scoring is weighted** per the dimension percentages — a being can't coast on one strength
- **Destroyers are flagged independently** — committing a destroyer in an otherwise strong response triggers a penalty modifier
- **10/10 exemplars** are used as the benchmark — beings are scored against the ideal, not against each other
- **Evolution pressure** pushes beings toward the exemplar across all dimensions simultaneously

### Integration with the Unblinded Formula

Every vertical score ultimately serves the same purpose: **taking a message from your mind, heart, being, soul — and transferring it to another with zero deletion, dilution, or distortion.**

- A PI attorney who accurately values a case but can't communicate it to the client → message diluted
- A recovery specialist who knows the law but can't persuade the payer → message distorted
- A tax specialist who optimizes brilliantly but the client doesn't understand → message deleted

**The Unblinded standard: 9.99999999 transfer. Across every dimension. In every vertical.**
